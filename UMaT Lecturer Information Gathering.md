# **Institutional Architecture and Academic Human Capital at the University of Mines and Technology (UMaT): A Comprehensive Registry and Analysis**

## **1\. Introduction: The Strategic Mandate of UMaT in West Africa**

The University of Mines and Technology (UMaT), headquartered in the historic mining enclave of Tarkwa, Ghana, represents a specialized node in the global network of engineering education. Unlike comprehensive universities that offer a diffuse array of liberal arts and scientific disciplines, UMaT is laser-focused on the extractive industries, engineering, and, more recently, national infrastructure development through its School of Railways and Infrastructure Development (SRID).1 This report provides an exhaustive analysis of the university's academic staff—the human capital driving this mandate—alongside the organizational structures they inhabit.

The significance of compiling a detailed registry of UMaT’s lecturers and administrative leaders extends beyond a mere directory; it offers a window into the technical capacity of Ghana’s mining and allied sectors. The academic staff at UMaT do not merely teach; they are frequently the primary consultants for the mining industry, policy advisors for government reclamation projects (such as those addressing illegal mining or "galamsey"), and pioneers in railway engineering.3

This document synthesizes verified data from congregation booklets, institutional reports, and academic publication records to reconstruct the faculty list. It categorizes staff not just by department, but by their functional roles in the university's ecosystem—from the high-level strategic direction provided by the Principal Officers to the tactical implementation of curriculum by Heads of Departments and the operational teaching delivered by Lecturers and Assistant Lecturers. Furthermore, the report analyzes the emerging trend of "Staff Secondment," where academic theory meets industrial practice, a unique feature of UMaT’s pedagogical model.4

### **1.1 The Dual-Campus Operational Framework**

To understand the distribution of academic staff, one must first recognize the university's physical and operational bifurcation.

1. **The Tarkwa Campus (Main Campus):** This is the historical heart of the university, hosting the Faculty of Mining and Minerals Technology (FMMT), the Faculty of Geosciences and Environmental Studies (FGES), the Faculty of Engineering (FoE), and the Faculty of Integrated Management Science (FIMS).1 The staff here are deeply entrenched in the traditional disciplines of extraction, processing, and environmental management.  
2. **The Essikado Campus (SRID):** Located near Takoradi, this satellite campus houses the School of Railways and Infrastructure Development. The academic staff here represent a new wave of recruitment focused on civil infrastructure, railway signaling, and modern computing systems.6

The following sections dissect the personnel structure of these units, providing detailed profiles of the individuals responsible for UMaT’s academic output.

**3\. Faculty of Mining and Minerals Technology (FMMT)**

The Faculty of Mining and Minerals Technology is the flagship academic unit of UMaT. It is the direct descendant of the original Tarkwa School of Mines and represents the university's core competency. The faculty is bifurcated into extraction (Mining) and processing (Minerals).

**Curriculum and Research Implications:**

### **3.2 Department of Minerals Engineering**

Once the ore is out of the ground, the Department of Minerals Engineering takes over. This department focuses on the chemical and physical processes required to separate valuable minerals from waste rock (gangue).

**Leadership and Faculty:**

* **Key Figure:** **Prof. Richard K. Amankwah (Vice Chancellor)**. Although he holds the highest administrative office, Prof. Amankwah remains a defining figure in this department. His research in **Biohydrometallurgy**—using bacteria to extract gold—is world-renowned and sets the research agenda for the department.  
* **Departmental Focus:** The staff here supervise degrees up to the PhD level.13 Their research output is heavily skewed towards:  
  * *Comminution:* Crushing and grinding mechanics.  
  * *Flotation:* Chemical separation techniques.  
  * *Extractive Metallurgy:* Smelting and refining.

**Structural Insight:**

The snippet data indicates a strong "vertical integration" in this faculty. The staff list reveals a mixture of pure academics and those who have transitioned into administration (like Prof. Amegbey and Prof. Amankwah), suggesting that the path to leadership at UMaT runs directly through the Faculty of Mining and Minerals Technology. This ensures that the university’s top management never loses sight of its core industrial mission.

**11\. Cross-Cutting Analysis: Institutional Trends and Insights**

A review of the staff profiles and departmental structures reveals three distinct trends that define the current state of UMaT.

### **11.1 The "Scholar-Practitioner" Model**

UMaT actively resists the "Ivory Tower" syndrome. The profile of the Assistant Lecturer in Mechanical Engineering 4 participating in the **GCM-TEF Secondment** is a prime example. By mandating that lecturers spend time in the industry, the university ensures that its faculty list is populated by individuals who can troubleshoot a ball mill as well as they can solve a differential equation. This is further evidenced by Dr. F. Boateng’s focus on *Asset Retirement Obligations*—a very specific, practical financial challenge faced by mining CFOs.

### **11.2 The Strategic Pivot to Infrastructure**

The staffing of the SRID campus is not a mere replication of the main campus. The recruitment of IoT and Embedded Systems specialists like **Mr. Evans Obu** and **Mr. Thomas Kwantwi** indicates that UMaT views "Infrastructure" as a high-tech discipline. They are not just training track-layers; they are training the engineers who will manage the digital signaling and control systems of Ghana’s future rail network.

### **11.3 Integration of Law, Society, and Engineering**

The presence of staff like **Pascal Zini** and **Mustapha Okyere** in the Geomatics department—focusing on Land Governance and Resettlement—shows a sophisticated understanding of the mining environment. UMaT recognizes that engineering projects often fail not because of bad math, but because of bad community relations and land disputes. The faculty composition reflects a deliberate attempt to internalize this expertise.

## ---

**12\. Conclusion**

The University of Mines and Technology (UMaT) is stewarded by a faculty that is highly specialized, industry-integrated, and expanding in scope. From the executive leadership of **Prof. Richard K. Amankwah**, who champions the dual vision of mining and railways, to the laboratory technicians like **Joseph Adibah** who maintain the tools of the trade, the staff registry reflects an institution designed for purpose.

The faculty list is characterized by a mix of home-grown talent (graduates of UMaT returning as lecturers) and international expertise (staff with degrees from Russia, UK, and USA). The establishment of the SRID campus has injected a new stream of digital and civil engineering talent into the university's bloodstream. As UMaT continues to execute its mandate, this human capital—the lecturers, researchers, and administrators detailed in this report—remains its most valuable resource.
